# Nutrition And Body Composition

This summary groups the papers most relevant to Spartan's diet coaching, body-composition strategy, and lean-mass retention logic.

## Why This Bucket Matters For Spartan

This bucket should strengthen:
- protein target logic
- cut vs maintenance vs lean-gain coaching defaults
- hydration and diet-quality caveats
- body-composition trend interpretation
- lean-mass retention under caloric restriction

## Source Buckets Included

- nutrition
- body-composition

## Main Questions To Answer

- What protein assumptions should Spartan use when direct intake evidence is incomplete?
- What should be the default coaching posture during cutting if training quality must remain high?
- How aggressive can weight-loss recommendations be before lean-mass risk rises too much?
- Which nutrition variables are most worth tracking operationally versus leaving as advice-only?

## Best-Supported Findings

### Protein Should Be Explicit, Not Vague

- The review in [s40279-025-02203-8.pdf](/Users/hd/Developer/cortana/research/raw/spartan/pdfs/s40279-025-02203-8.pdf) argues that athletic protein needs are often around `1.8 g/kg/day`, with needs potentially exceeding `2.0 g/kg/day` during low-carb or low-energy phases.
- That same paper supports a per-meal intake around `0.5 g/kg` as a useful recovery target.

Spartan implication:
- Spartan should set explicit daily protein floors and meal-level targets instead of generic “eat more protein” advice.

### Creatine Is A High-Confidence Default Supplement

- The systematic review and dose-response meta-analysis in [Creatine_supplementation_and_r.pdf](/Users/hd/Developer/cortana/research/raw/spartan/pdfs/Creatine_supplementation_and_r.pdf) found significant increases in fat-free mass and body mass with creatine plus resistance training.
- The gains were not explained by meaningful fat-loss effects.

Spartan implication:
- Creatine should be treated as a baseline recommendation for strength and hypertrophy support unless there is a reason not to use it.

### During Cuts, Lean-Mass Retention Should Dominate

- The invited review in [s00421-022-04896-5.pdf](/Users/hd/Developer/cortana/research/raw/spartan/pdfs/s00421-022-04896-5.pdf) suggests higher resistance-training volume helps preserve lean mass during caloric restriction, especially when weekly productive sets remain meaningfully present.
- The direction of evidence favors maintaining or even increasing productive volume rather than automatically cutting training stress too hard.

Spartan implication:
- A cut should not automatically mean a big drop in lifting stimulus.
- Spartan should protect muscle retention as a first-class objective.

### Keto Should Be Niche, Not Default

- The position stand in [International society of sports nutrition position stand  ketogenic diets.pdf](/Users/hd/Developer/cortana/research/raw/spartan/pdfs/International%20society%20of%20sports%20nutrition%20position%20stand%20%20ketogenic%20diets.pdf) reports that ketogenic diets are often neutral or negative for performance versus higher-carbohydrate diets.
- Body-weight and fat-mass reductions can occur, but lean-mass retention and performance can suffer.

Spartan implication:
- Keto should be a narrow opt-in experiment with explicit tradeoffs, not the default Spartan diet posture.

### Education And Adherence Matter

- The intervention in [fpubh-12-1289448.pdf](/Users/hd/Developer/cortana/research/raw/spartan/pdfs/fpubh-12-1289448.pdf) improved energy availability, nutrition knowledge, and fat-free mass in young female athletes.
- The powerlifter survey in [s00394-023-03233-6.pdf](/Users/hd/Developer/cortana/research/raw/spartan/pdfs/s00394-023-03233-6.pdf) suggests many lifters already eat more on hard training days and often use flexible dieting patterns.

Spartan implication:
- Coaching should teach, reinforce, and phase fueling, not only set static macros.

## Practical Spartan Rules To Test

1. Default daily protein to about `1.8 g/kg/day`, with higher targets in cuts or other high-demand phases.
2. Add creatine as a baseline recommendation for muscle and performance support.
3. During cuts, protect lean mass by preserving productive resistance-training volume.
4. Keep carbohydrate-supportive nutrition as the default performance posture.
5. Build adherence and fueling education into the coaching loop rather than assuming static targets will be followed.

## Confidence

- Higher confidence:
  - protein deserves explicit operational targets
  - creatine is a strong default recommendation
  - lean-mass retention should be actively protected during cuts
  - keto is not the default athletic-performance diet
- Moderate confidence:
  - exact protein upper bounds by phase
  - exact weekly set thresholds needed to preserve lean mass during cuts
- Lower confidence:
  - broader gut-health or antioxidant policies from the current corpus
